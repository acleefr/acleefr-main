---
title: The Nicolas Problem
date: December 24, 2025
excerpt: Two thirds of French people receive more in public transfers than they ever pay in taxes. That's not a welfare state — that's a structural dependency that no one in French politics wants to name.
---

There's a line that's been floating around French political debate for a while now: *"2/3 des Français reçoivent plus d'impôts qu'ils n'en paient dans leur vie."* Two thirds of French people receive more from the state than they ever contribute to it in taxes.

That's not a critique. That's just arithmetic.

And it maps pretty cleanly onto something I think about a lot: there are roughly 1/3 of people carrying the system, and 2/3 depending on it. I call the dependent majority "Nicolas" — not as an insult, but as a useful shorthand for the median French voter who benefits from the status quo and has every rational incentive to defend it.

### The System "Works" — Until It Doesn't

The usual response to this is: *"But the system functions, doesn't it?"*

No. Not really. Not sustainably.

French public spending is only defensible as a model if you assume perpetual reform and perpetual growth. Neither of those things has materialized in the last twenty years. What we have instead is a system that has been rolling forward on debt and deferred decisions, with each government kicking the structural problems down the road for someone else to deal with.

The math is not mysterious. It is just politically inconvenient.

### The "You Benefited Too" Argument

Another classic: *"But you were born in a public hospital. You went to a subsidized school."*

Yes. And only 8.8% of public spending goes to education. Only 8.4% goes to hospitals.

So when people invoke public services to justify the full scale of French fiscal pressure, they are conflating the parts of the state that actually deliver value with the enormous share of spending that goes elsewhere — transfers, bureaucracy, subsidies, structural inefficiencies.

You can believe in public education and public healthcare and still think the overall allocation is broken. Those are not the same debate.

### On the 211 Billion for Corporations

This one comes up constantly. *"Private companies received 211 billion in public money."*

This is a misreading. Most of that figure is tax relief — notably the *Crédit d'Impôt pour la Compétitivité et l'Emploi* and similar mechanisms. That is not the state writing checks to corporations. It is the state taxing them less than it otherwise would.

The distinction matters. If you believe the baseline rate of taxation is legitimate and any deviation from it is a gift, then yes, it's a subsidy. But if you think taxation is the deviation from a default of letting people and businesses keep what they earn, then it's a different framing entirely.

### What Actually Needs to Happen

France does not have a revenue problem. It has a spending structure problem. The question is not how to extract more from the 1/3 who already carry the system. The question is how to reduce the dependency ratio — more people producing, fewer people structurally reliant on transfers — without dismantling the parts of the public sector that actually work.

That requires a political conversation that French democracy has systematically refused to have. Because Nicolas votes. And Nicolas votes for whoever promises to leave the transfers untouched.

This is not sustainable. It is just slow.

— Arthur Benjamin Clément
