Slide makers are dead.

Not the people. The old role.

Making presentations used to mean spending a huge chunk of time on production: layouts, alignment, spacing, formatting, exports, versions. You'd write some notes, open the tool, and then disappear into an hour of manual work before anything looked presentable. The thinking got buried under the doing.

AI changes that loop.

### The New Workflow

The new workflow is not "ask AI for slides and trust the result." That's not it.

It is: **goal → story → structure → visuals → iteration → final judgment.**

The human stays responsible for meaning, taste, facts, and decisions. AI accelerates the production work around it. You still have to know what you want to say. You still have to catch what's wrong. You still have to make the final call.

But the slow, repetitive middle part — the part that used to eat your afternoon — compresses dramatically.

### What the Old Loop Actually Looked Like

Old workflow: write notes, create slides, align objects, resize text, rebuild versions, export, repeat.

New workflow: write the intent, review the structure, ask for visual directions, iterate fast, export clean outputs.

Less manual production. More thinking time. The bottleneck moves from *making* to *deciding*.

### What I Like About This

The process leaves artifacts. A plan for the story. A visual source of truth. Reusable assets. Exported files ready for sharing.

The work stays editable instead of disappearing inside a chat. That matters. You can come back to it, hand it off, adjust it. It's not a one-shot prompt that you lose the moment you close the window.

### The Real Lesson

AI does not kill presentation work.

It kills the slow, repetitive slide-making bottleneck.

That changes the job from "make slides" to "direct the message." Which, honestly, is what it should have always been.

— Arthur Benjamin Clément
