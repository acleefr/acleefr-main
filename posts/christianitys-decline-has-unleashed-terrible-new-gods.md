*A review of Madeline Grant's article, originally published in The Telegraph.*

Not since the road to Damascus has there been a more notable spiritual volte-face than the one made on LBC. Having spent a career breathing threats against the disciples of the Lord, a certain Richard Dawkins is struck by a moment of realisation.

Professor Dawkins' admission that he considers himself a "cultural Christian", who is, at the very least, ambivalent about Anglicanism's decline is an undeniably contradictory position for a man who in the past campaigned relentlessly against any role for Christianity in public life.

Still, though Dawkins has spoken of his "cultural Christianity" before, this feels like another staging-post on a journey towards admitting that the New Atheism was wrong in crucial respects. First, in its almost touching naivety that a post-Christian world would give way to a values-neutral space, rooted in reason. Second, in its semi-adolescent diagnosis of Christianity as a retardant upon cultural and intellectual progress.

### The Rise of New Dogmas

It doesn't take the brains of an evolutionary biologist to work out that New Atheism was mistaken in its diagnosis of what would follow religion's decline. The rational world we were promised hasn't materialised, and a nastier, less reasonable one is supplanting what was there before.

Much of what atheists ascribed to vague concepts of "reason" emerged out of the faith which informed the West's intellectual, moral, and scientific life — a cultural oxygen we breathe but never see. Recognizing Christianity's cultural impact is the first step. The bigger task facing the West is living out these values in an age when they are increasingly under threat.
