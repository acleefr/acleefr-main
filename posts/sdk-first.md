We're currently building an SDK at Diabolocom. And the more I work on it, the more I'm convinced that shipping an SDK is a fundamentally different business than shipping a UI — and a much bigger one.

Let me explain the difference using oil.

### Crude vs. Gas

When you drill oil, you have two options. You can refine it yourself, build the stations, hire the staff, design the nozzle, and sell gas directly to drivers. That's a real business. People see your brand, they come back, it's tangible.

Or you can sell the crude. Let everyone else build their stations, their trucks, their distribution networks. You sit upstream. Every refinery that opens is a new customer. Every gas station that serves a million drivers is pulling from your barrel.

A UI-first product is the gas station. Useful. Visible. Capped.

An SDK is the crude. Less visible. Infinitely scalable.

When you sell a UI, you're solving one problem, one way, for one type of user. When you sell an SDK, you're selling the raw capability — and your customers go solve ten problems you never thought of, for users you'd never have reached, in products you'd never have built.

The leverage is completely different. You're not serving end users. You're enabling builders who serve end users. The multiplier is enormous.

### Don't Kill the UI Though

Here's where I'd push back on pure SDK religion: you still need to ship a UI. Not instead of the SDK. Alongside it.

The UI does three things the SDK can't. It proves your own product works. It gives non-technical buyers something to evaluate. And it generates the real-world feedback that makes the SDK better — because you're dogfooding your own abstraction, constantly.

If you only ship the SDK, you're asking customers to trust a black box. The UI is the live demo that never ends. It's evidence.

The builders who try your UI become your best SDK adopters. The enterprise buyer who needs a proof-of-concept can see one in five minutes. The integration partner who's deciding whether to bet on your API gets to watch it run.

UI and SDK are not competing priorities. The UI sells the SDK. The SDK scales the UI.

### Why This Matters Now

Building a UI used to be a meaningful chunk of engineering time. Design, frontend, state management, component libraries, responsiveness, edge cases — weeks of work before you had something shippable.

That equation has changed. AI has compressed the UI layer dramatically. A competent team can ship a working interface in days that would have taken months two years ago. The UI is no longer the hard part.

Which means the actual product — the real differentiation, the thing that's hard to copy — has moved. It's in the backend. In the data model. In the API design. In the SDK ergonomics. In the abstraction that makes your capability feel inevitable to the developer who picks it up.

The UI is table stakes. The SDK is the moat.

### What We're Building

At Diabolocom, working on the SDK means thinking about this constantly. What does the developer experience feel like? What's the minimum surface area that unlocks maximum flexibility? Where does the SDK end and the product begin?

These are harder questions than "what should this button do." They require a different kind of thinking — upstream, architectural, long-term. You're not designing for the user in front of you. You're designing for the builder who doesn't exist yet, building something you haven't imagined.

That's the interesting problem. And it's the one that scales.

— Arthur Benjamin Clément
